USA Football Guide

Your complete overview of how American football works — rules, scoring, teams, and the full season breakdown.

1. Introduction to American Football

American football is widely considered the most popular sport in the United States, drawing millions of viewers for professional and collegiate games alike. It is a game of intense physical strategy, often described as "chess with pads," where two teams of 11 players compete to move an oval-shaped ball down a 100-yard field to score points. The game combines explosive athleticism with complex tactical planning, making it a unique spectacle in the sporting world.

Unlike continuous-play sports like soccer, football is played in short bursts of intense action known as "plays." Between plays, teams have a brief period to substitute players and strategize for the next move. This stop-and-start nature allows for a high degree of specialization among players and coaches.

2. The Objective and The Field

The primary objective is to score more points than the opponent within the allotted time. The game is played on a rectangular field that is 120 yards long (including two 10-yard end zones) and 53.3 yards wide. The field is marked with yard lines every 5 yards, and hash marks indicate where the ball can be placed laterally.

The game is controlled by the concept of Downs. The offense (the team with the ball) has four attempts, or "downs," to advance the ball at least 10 yards. If they succeed, they are awarded a new set of four downs (a "First Down"). If they fail to gain 10 yards after four attempts, possession turns over to the defense at the current spot of the ball. This system creates the dramatic tension that defines the sport.

3. Scoring Breakdown

Points can be scored in several ways, each with different strategic implications:

  • Touchdown (6 Points): The most valuable score. It is achieved when a player carries the ball across the opponent's goal line into the end zone or catches a pass while standing within the end zone.
  • Extra Point (1 Point): Immediately following a touchdown, the scoring team can kick the ball through the goalposts from a short distance. This is the most common choice.
  • Two-Point Conversion (2 Points): Instead of kicking, the team can attempt one offensive play from the 2-yard line to get the ball into the end zone again. This is riskier but offers a higher reward.
  • Field Goal (3 Points): If the offense cannot reach the end zone but is within kicking range (typically inside the 35-yard line), they may attempt to kick the ball through the uprights.
  • Safety (2 Points): A rare score awarded to the defense if they tackle an offensive ball-carrier inside the offense's own end zone. The defense also receives possession of the ball via a free kick afterwards.

4. Player Positions and Roles

Football teams are divided into three specialized units: Offense, Defense, and Special Teams.

The Offense

  • Quarterback (QB): The leader of the team. He calls the plays, receives the snap, and either hands the ball off or passes it downfield.
  • Offensive Line (OL): Consisting of the Center, Guards, and Tackles, these large players block defenders to protect the QB and open lanes for runners.
  • Running Back (RB): Specialized in carrying the ball on running plays. They must be durable, fast, and elusive.
  • Wide Receiver (WR): Speed specialists who run specific routes to catch passes from the quarterback.
  • Tight End (TE): A hybrid position requiring the strength to block like a lineman and the agility to catch passes like a receiver.

The Defense

  • Defensive Line (DL): Positioned directly opposite the offensive line, their job is to disrupt the play, tackle runners, and pressure the quarterback.
  • Linebackers (LB): Positioned behind the defensive line, they are versatile defenders who tackle runners and cover receivers.
  • Defensive Backs (DB): Cornerbacks and Safeties cover wide receivers to prevent completed passes and provide the last line of defense against long runs.

5. Common Penalties

Referees enforce strict rules to ensure fair play and safety. Common infractions include:

  • Holding: Illegally grabbing or pulling an opponent (other than the ball carrier) to prevent them from moving. (10-yard penalty).
  • False Start: When an offensive player moves before the ball is snapped. (5-yard penalty).
  • Offside: When a defensive player crosses the line of scrimmage before the snap. (5-yard penalty).
  • Pass Interference: Illegally hindering a player's ability to catch a pass while the ball is in the air.

6. The Season Structure

The professional football season in the U.S. follows a rigorous schedule:

  • Preseason (August): Exhibition games used to evaluate players and finalize the roster.
  • Regular Season (September - January): Teams play a schedule of 17 games over 18 weeks. Performance here determines playoff eligibility.
  • Playoffs (January): A single-elimination tournament featuring the top teams from each conference.
  • The Championship (February): The final game of the season, one of the most-watched television events in history, determining the league champion.
